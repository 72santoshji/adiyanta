ADIYANTA

That which exists before the beginning
and remains after the end.

This is not a book.
It is a sitting.

Before the Fire — the Chit Became Witness

The fire was lit.
Not violent.
Not calm.
Simply burning.

Flames rose upward — slow, steady,
as if time itself had forgotten how to move.

Smoke drifted toward the sky,
but within me something else was gathering weight.

This was a cremation.
Yet it did not feel like an ending.
It felt like the dissolution of form.

People stood around.
Words were spoken.
Rituals continued.

But for me,
everything was slowly dissolving into silence.

Swāhā.
Each time the word fell into the fire,
something else fell with it.

A name.
A shape.
An identity.
What had been called “I.”

The fire accepted everything.
Without question.
Without resistance.

I was not listening to chants.
I was not watching ceremony.
I was watching how everything returns.

Wood was being offered to the fire.
Form was changing.
Existence was not.

In that moment,
my chit became a witness.
Not a thinker.
Not a judge.
Only a seer.

And without words,
the first movement arose.
Not a question.
Not curiosity.
A remembering.

As if something ancient within me had awakened.

The fire was burning.
Yet it was not burning itself.
It was a medium.

Wood transformed —
into smoke,
into ash,
into air.

But the fire…
remained only the cause.

The chit did not give an answer.
It gave a direction.

“यत् सर्वं भवति, पर स्वयं न भवति।”
(That because of which everything happens, yet which itself does not happen.)

My eyes were on the fire.
But my awareness had shifted elsewhere.

What is false burns and disappears.
What is true remains unseen,
untouched,
even while burning.

I stayed seated.
No conclusion.
No doctrine.
Only fire. Chit. Silence.

I did not search for answers.
I did not seek belief.
I did not seek comfort.

I asked myself ten questions.
Not aloud.
Not in sequence.

They did not demand answers.
They demanded honesty.

This book is not the answers to those questions.
It is the path they opened.

This is where Adiyanta begins.
